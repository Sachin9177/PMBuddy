# PMBuddy Daily Digest — March 31, 2026
**Domain: AI and SaaS**

### 🧠 1. Key Trend / Insight
**AI is shifting from features to workflows — and SaaS pricing models haven't caught up**

For the past two years, SaaS companies bolted AI onto existing products as features: "AI-powered summaries," "smart suggestions," "copilot mode." That phase is ending.

The next wave is AI-native workflows — products where the AI *is* the product, not an add-on. Think Cursor (the IDE built around AI-assisted coding, not an AI plugin for VS Code) versus GitHub Copilot (AI added to an existing tool). The former is winning adoption among developers who want to go all-in.

The pricing problem: most SaaS companies still charge per seat. But if an AI agent can do the work of five users, per-seat pricing collapses. Companies like Intercom and Zendesk are already experimenting with outcome-based pricing — charging per resolved ticket rather than per agent seat.

**What PMs should do differently:** Stop thinking about AI as a feature to add to your roadmap. Ask instead: if AI could own one full workflow end-to-end, which one would have the highest impact? Then design backward from that. Also pressure-test your pricing model — if your AI works well, does it cannibalize your own revenue?

### 🚀 2. Product Case Study
**Linear — How a project management tool won over engineers by doing less**

- **Company & feature:** Linear, a project management SaaS targeting software teams, launched with a stripped-down feature set compared to Jira and Asana — no Gantt charts, no custom field overload, no enterprise approval workflows.
- **The problem they were solving:** Engineers hated using project management tools. They were slow, cluttered, and felt built for managers, not the people actually doing the work. Ticket creation friction was so high that teams stopped updating their boards, making the tools useless.
- **What they did:** Linear made speed the core product value. Keyboard-first navigation, sub-100ms interactions, a clean opinionated data model (issues → projects → cycles), and a design that felt like a developer tool, not enterprise software. They said no to a huge number of features for years.
- **Why it worked:** They picked a specific user (engineers) and optimized entirely for what that user valued (speed, clarity, no bloat). Most tools try to satisfy all stakeholders — PM, eng, design, leadership — and end up satisfying no one deeply. Linear made a bet that if engineers loved the tool, adoption would follow.
- **Takeaway for PMs:** "Who is this product *really* for?" is not a rhetorical question. When you have multiple user types, you often have to pick one to optimize for first. Trying to please all of them equally is a product strategy, but it's usually a losing one.

### 🧩 3. PM Framework / Concept
**Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) — but the part most PMs miss**

- **Name:** Jobs-to-be-Done, specifically the "struggling moment"
- **What it is:** People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job when they hit a moment of friction or desire. The classic example: people don't buy a drill, they hire it to make a hole. But the part most PMs skip is that the job has a *trigger* — a specific moment when the struggle becomes acute enough to act. That trigger is where the real insight lives.
- **When to use it:** User interviews, discovery research, positioning decisions, and onboarding design. Especially useful when you're trying to understand why users churn or why adoption is low despite good retention signals.
- **When NOT to use it:** Don't use JTBD to justify building features you've already decided to build. It's a discovery tool, not a validation rubber stamp. Also weak for understanding habitual behavior — JTBD is best for understanding switching moments, not ongoing use.
- **Quick example:** A SaaS company discovers through JTBD interviews that users don't sign up because they want "better reporting" — they sign up the day after a bad board meeting where their CEO asked for numbers they couldn't produce. That trigger changes how you write onboarding copy and which features you surface first.

### ⚡ 4. Practical Tip
**Before your next sprint planning, write a one-sentence "so that" for every ticket.**

Most engineering tickets describe *what* to build, not *why*. "Add filter to the dashboard" tells the team what to do but not what outcome it drives.

Force yourself to complete: *"We are building [X] so that [specific user outcome]."*

- "We are adding a filter to the dashboard so that ops managers can isolate daily booking data without exporting to a spreadsheet."

If you can't write the "so that" in one sentence, the ticket isn't ready to build. This single habit prevents your sprint from filling up with work that ships but doesn't move any metric.

The common mistake this prevents: shipping features that are used once and abandoned because no one validated the actual need before building.

### 📰 5. What's Happening
**Salesforce is betting its future on "Agentforce" — autonomous AI agents inside CRM**

Salesforce announced and began rolling out Agentforce, a platform for deploying AI agents that autonomously handle CRM tasks — qualifying leads, resolving support tickets, updating records — without human initiation. It's a direct response to the threat that standalone AI tools (like Claude or GPT-based wrappers) could displace CRM workflows entirely.

**Why it matters for product people:** This is a major SaaS incumbent trying to evolve its core product before AI disrupts it. The interesting strategic question is whether bolting agents onto existing CRM architecture works, or whether the data model and UX assumptions of legacy CRM are fundamentally incompatible with agentic workflows. Early signals are mixed — enterprises are buying it, but developer adoption is cautious.

**The signal:** Every SaaS company with a workflow-heavy product will face the same question Salesforce is facing now. The ones that wait for clarity will be too late.

### 🧠 6. Thinking Question

**If your AI feature works perfectly and reduces the time users spend in your product by 60%, is that a success or a threat to your business?**

Most engagement metrics assume more time in product = more value delivered. AI inverts this. A user who gets what they need in 2 minutes and leaves is arguably a better outcome than one who spends 20 minutes clicking around — but your retention dashboard might not know the difference.

How would you measure value if time-in-product is no longer a valid proxy? And if your pricing is seat-based, what happens to your revenue model when AI makes each seat dramatically more efficient?

**Sit with this for 5 minutes before your next meeting.**
