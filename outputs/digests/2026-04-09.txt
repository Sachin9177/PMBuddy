### 📘 3. Product Case Study

**Stripe — Adaptive Pricing for Subscription Checkout**

- **Company:** Stripe
- **What they did:** In early 2026, Stripe shipped Adaptive Pricing for subscription checkout — automatically localizing prices across 150+ countries and handling currency conversion, then validated it with an A/B test across 1.5 million subscription checkout sessions.
- **Why it worked:** The product changed the *default behavior* at the moment of purchase: instead of showing a USD price in local currency, it showed a price optimized for local purchasing power. The A/B test gave conversion signal at scale rather than shipping on intuition, which meant the team could tie the configuration change directly to revenue impact before evangelizing it internally.
- **PM takeaway:** Default behaviors compound harder than opt-in features — when you control the flow, changing what happens when a user does nothing (rather than adding a toggle they'll never find) is almost always the higher-leverage move.

### 🧠 4. PM Framework / Concept

- **Name:** Outcomes vs. Outputs
- **The idea:** An output is the thing you shipped; an outcome is the user behavior change that justified shipping it — and only outcomes validate roadmap spend.
- **When to use it:** When a stakeholder requests a specific feature instead of naming the problem, or when your team is celebrating a launch that didn't move the metric it was supposed to move.
- **Example:** A team redesigns onboarding (output) and ships on time. But if 60-day retention doesn't improve, there's no outcome — the roadmap slot was consumed by activity, not progress. The correct framing from the start: "We want 55% of new users to complete their first project within 48 hours — what's the minimum change that gets us there?" That question rules out the redesign and points to the activation step that's actually failing.

### ⚡ 6. Practical Tip

- **The tip:** Before optimizing how you respond to notifications, build a triage layer — route all incoming async signals (Slack mentions, pings, alerts) into a single batched digest with explicit actionability labels, and only process them on a schedule.
- **When to use it:** When you're a PM managing multiple concurrent workstreams and find yourself reacting to the most recent message rather than the highest-priority problem.
- **Why it works:** Yash Tekriwal at Clay reduced 150 daily Slack notifications to ~30 actionable tasks by building a digest with OpenAI agents that classified messages by type before surfacing them. The failure mode it prevents is specific: without a filter, you optimize *response latency* (replying fast) instead of *decision quality* (acting on what matters). Most notifications are informational — conflating them with action items burns the same cognitive budget as actual decisions, just without the output.
